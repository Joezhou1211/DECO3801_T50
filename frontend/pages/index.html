The Impacts of the Bush Fires

The 2019-2020 bushfire season has seen more than 18 million hectares burnt across the country, destroyed with 5,900 buildings destroyed, including 2,800 homes and the deaths of 34 people and approximately one billion animals. Whilst these are the 'headline statistics' considered when assessing the extent of the damage caused by the fires, especially by the public, on further analysis, the impacts are more far-reaching and severe than even the initially shocking figures would suggest.

18 Million Hectares Burned

The bushfires scorched vast areas of land across Australia, leaving destruction in their wake.

5,900 Buildings Destroyed

Thousands of homes and buildings were destroyed, causing billions in damages.

1 Billion Animals Died

The fires devastated local wildlife, causing significant losses to biodiversity.

Social Impacts

Explore the far-reaching social impacts of the bushfires:

  • 18 million hectares burned
  • 5,900 buildings destroyed, 2,800 homes
  • 1 billion animals killed
  • 34 people killed directly
  • Public health impacts: Poor air quality - Canberra measured the worst air quality index of any major city in the world.
  • Wildfires produce harmful smoke which can cause fatalities - asthma, bronchitis, etc.
  • Estimated 417 deaths due to poor air quality
  • Mental health impacts: Trauma of evacuation, losing pets, homes, belongings.
  • Some communities found themselves unable to evacuate due to power loss or blocked roads, leading to lasting mental health effects.

Economic Impacts

  • Infrastructure damage impacts industries such as farming and tourism.
  • Businesses and institutions closed during periods of excessive air pollution.
  • Economic costs expected to exceed the $4.4 billion cost of the 2009 'Black Saturday' fires (Source: Guardian).

Environmental Impacts

Here are some key environmental consequences of the 2019-2020 bushfires:

  • Ash from fires kills crops and pollutes water, leading to algal blooms in streams and rivers.
  • Loss of biodiversity due to habitat destruction.
  • Climate feedback loops: Forests previously absorbed carbon emissions, but due to scale, this is no longer achievable.
  • 400 megatonnes of carbon dioxide emitted into the atmosphere during the 2019-2020 fires.

Misleading Maps and Information Going Viral

Some celebrities used social media as a tool for raising awareness about the fires to raise funds, one of the most viral tweets linked to this was from singer Rhianna who posted an image of Australia covered in lava like markings meant to show where active fires were. This was revealed however to be an inaccurate visualisation of the crisis, despite using NASA data to make the map. The artist of the map is Anthony Hearsay, who used a months worth of fire data overlaid over each other to create a compilation of the blazes, this process made it seem like more of Australia was on fire at one time. The map was shared and retweeted by thousands, fear mongering large groups of people and possibly pushing more people to donate to the cause than normal. It questions the morals of humanitarian appeals and fundraising if they are set on false data despite ultimately helping those in need.

Final Insights

This project has aimed to explore matters of causation and impact, historical context, humanitarian response and the impact of social media on the most recent bush fire season in Australia, which appeared to be notable more catastrophic and present in global public consciousness than ever before.

The fires were caused by a combination of long term climatic causes that fostered the appropriate conditions, notably an arid environment , that allowed the potential for lightning strikes to ignite these areas. The Impacts of this event were noticeably severe and extensive, even in comparison to prior examples of damaging fires such as 2009's 'Black Saturday' blazes. Therefore it is evident that the 2019-2020 bush fire season represented a disaster on an unrivalled scale.

This historical perspective shows us that the bush fires in Australia have always been a part of the landscape that has shaped the culture of this ancient land. Even before the first European colonisers began to arrive towards the end of the eighteenth century, for many generations, indigenous peoples have continued to face devastation that bush fires have caused.
