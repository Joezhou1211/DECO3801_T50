The Impacts of the Bush Fires

The 2019-2020 bushfire season has seen more than 18 million hectares burnt across the country, destroyed with 5,900 buildings destroyed, including 2,800 homes and the deaths of 34 people and approximately one billion animals. Whilst these are the 'headline statistics' considered when assessing the extent of the damage caused by the fires, especially by the public, on further analysis, the impacts are more far-reaching and severe than even the initially shocking figures would suggest.

The Social Impacts (Source: UN Environment Programme)
  • 18 million hectares burned
  • 5,900 buildings destroyed - 2,800 homes
  • 1 billion Animals killed
  • 34 people killed directly
  • Public health impacts: Poor air quality - Canberra measured the worst air quality index of any major city in the world.
  • Wildfires produce harmful smoke which can cause fatalities - asthma, bronchitis, etc.
  • Estimated 417 deaths due to poor air quality (Source: Medical Journal of Australia)
  • Mental health impacts: Trauma of evacuation, losing pets, homes, belongings.
  • Some communities found themselves unable to evacuate due to power loss or blocked roads, leading to lasting mental health effects.
Economic Impacts
  • Infrastructure damage impacts industries such as farming and tourism.
  • Businesses and institutions closed during periods of excessive air pollution.
  • Economic costs expected to exceed the $4.4 billion cost of the 2009 'Black Saturday' fires (Source: Guardian).
Environmental Impacts
  • Ash from fires kills crops and pollutes water, leading to algal blooms in streams and rivers.
  • Loss of biodiversity due to habitat destruction.
  • Climate feedback loops: Forests previously absorbed carbon emissions, but due to scale, this is no longer achievable.
  • 400 megatonnes of carbon dioxide emitted into the atmosphere during the 2019-2020 fires (Source: Copernicus monitoring programme).
